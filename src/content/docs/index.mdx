How do you let an LLM talk to a sensitive database without leaking it? Most of the writing on this question is either marketing ("our platform is secure") or a single-issue rant about prompt injection. Neither helps the engineer who has to ship something next week.

This handbook is a reference. It names the mental models that make LLM-data security tractable, catalogues the attacks worth knowing, lays out an architecture you can copy, and tells you which controls to use under which conditions.

<Aside type="danger" title="The one rule, if you only read one thing">
**The lethal trifecta.** Any system that combines (1) access to private data, (2) exposure to untrusted input, and (3) an exfiltration channel back to an attacker is extractable. Remove any one of the three and the risk drops by an order of magnitude. Almost every real defense in this handbook is a way of breaking one of these three legs.
</Aside>

## What this handbook is not

It is not a survey of every LLM-security paper, not a vendor comparison, not a defence of any one architecture. Where research-grade techniques (CaMeL, StruQ, SecAlign) belong on your radar but aren't yet ready for production, they're called out as **Ausblick** — outlook, not playbook.

It also is not a substitute for reading [Simon Willison's blog](https://simonwillison.net/), the [MCP specification](https://modelcontextprotocol.io), or the [OWASP LLM Top 10](https://genai.owasp.org/llm-top-10/). It does intend to be the document you reach for first when one of those isn't sufficient on its own.
