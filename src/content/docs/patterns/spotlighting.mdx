Spotlighting is a family of prompt-engineering techniques introduced by Hines et al. (Microsoft, 2024)[^sp]. The premise: the LLM cannot structurally distinguish instructions from data ([control plane vs data plane](../mental-models/02-control-data-plane/)) — but you can make the distinction *visible* via formatting, marking, or transformation, and rely on the model's training to weight that signal.

## The three variants

- **Delimiting.** Wrap untrusted spans in markers — XML-like tags, fenced code blocks, custom tokens — and instruct the model to treat content inside as data only. Easiest to implement, weakest of the three (an injection can simply close the marker).
- **Datamarking.** Replace every space inside untrusted text with an unusual but consistent marker (e.g. `^`). The model is reminded throughout the span that it is reading marked content. Stronger than delimiting because the marker survives within the text.
- **Encoding.** Encode untrusted text (base64, ROT13) before placing it in the prompt; instruct the model to decode-and-process. Strongest of the three; the cost is that the model has to understand the encoding well enough to operate on the content.

The original paper reports attack-success-rate reductions from over 50% to under 2% on GPT-family models with the encoding variant, with minimal impact on legitimate-task performance.

## Why it's an Ausblick

Spotlighting is a partial, prompt-engineering defence. It does not change [capability](../mental-models/04-capability-over-intent/); the model can still be jailbroken and can still emit any tool call its surface allows. What it does is reduce the *frequency* of successful indirect injection — useful when you cannot change the model and cannot eliminate untrusted-data exposure.

It is worth knowing about because (a) it is genuinely cheap to add, (b) the result is measurable, and (c) it composes well with capability constraints. Use it as a probability-shifter on top of structural defences, not in place of them.

## Decision: when to use this

- **Worth trying if** you have a known indirect-injection surface (RAG documents, retrieved emails, free-text DB fields) and want to reduce the rate at which the model gets confused.
- **Skip if** you have already eliminated the exfiltration channel via [mediation](./mediated-tool-surface/) — the additional value is marginal.
- **Combine with** datamarking on free-text fields you must show the model.

[^sp]: Hines, K., et al. (2024). *Defending Against Indirect Prompt Injection Attacks With Spotlighting*. [arXiv:2403.14720](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.14720).
