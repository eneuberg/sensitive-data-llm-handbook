A reference catalog. Each entry follows a consistent shape:

- **What it is** — one paragraph.
- **How it works** — the concrete mechanism.
- **Where it dies in this architecture** — the layer or pattern that defeats it (with a link).
- **Reference** — academic source or notable real-world incident, when applicable.

If you arrive here from an incident, scan the titles in the sidebar; the attack you're looking at almost certainly belongs to one of these families.

## How to use this catalog

- **Designing a system?** Walk this list and ask, for each entry, *"would this attack succeed against my design?"*. Anything you can't answer crisply is a hole.
- **Investigating an incident?** Find the matching entry, then jump to the linked defence to see what should have stopped it.
- **Reading a paper or vendor pitch?** Map any "novel attack" claim back to the family it belongs to. Most are variants.

## What's not catalogued here

This is not OWASP. It is the subset of attacks that actually matter for *sensitive-data* systems specifically. Excluded:

- Attacks against the model's *output quality* unrelated to data leakage (hallucination, bias, harmful content). Important — different handbook.
- Attacks against the LLM provider's infrastructure. Out of scope.
- Attacks specific to multimodal / vision / audio inputs unless they have a database-relevant exfiltration path.

For the broader landscape, see [OWASP LLM Top 10](https://genai.owasp.org/llm-top-10/), [MITRE ATLAS](https://atlas.mitre.org/), and the [further reading](../appendix/further-reading/) appendix.
